CLOB · Order-book matching

A separate family: orders match against quotes posted by active MMs. Flexible — MMs can reprice on any model and capture skew arbitrage — but requires sustained MM presence. Without active MMs, the market goes dark.

AMM · curve-priced & pool-funded

Eight pool-based models. The LP commits capital to a pool, the protocol prices every trade from a rule (curve, fixed-odds quote, or redistribution mechanism), and the LP eats whatever the rule mispriced. Differ by what the LP holds at resolution, how much they can lose, and whether they earn anything in expectation.

How to read the Sim P&L column ·  Each return is the mean LP return across 30 random match seeds on a $100,000 vault, 25 micro-moments per match, 4% informed flow share. The "winning matches" count is how many of those 30 seeds left the LP with a positive return. Two categories produce positive returns: fixed-odds books with a live odds feed (Game Vault) and parimutuel (LP doesn't take risk). Every curve-based AMM loses in expectation against the informed share, even with the best-known mechanism math.

Recommended launch model

Game Vault Market Maker

One match-level LP vault. An odds engine prices each micro-moment from a live probability model, adds vig, and skews odds for the vault's own exposure. The vault takes the other side of every bet — it's an LP-funded sportsbook book, not an AMM.

Who takes the riskTHE VAULT — an LP-funded house warehouses every bet, prices it, caps it, and charges vig for it

Inputs

Why this winsThe vault behaves like a house: it quotes from a real probability model, pockets vig on every market, pays winners and absorbs losers from one shared pot across all 25 micro-moments. No per-market cold start, and exposure-skewed odds plus liability caps keep any single match from blowing the vault. The risk it can't dodge is informed flow inside the latency window — see the CPMM and the latency note.
Rejected

CPMM — constant product

A fresh YES/NO pool seeds at 50/50 and prices purely off inventory: x·y = k. No probability model — the price only moves because someone traded. Watch the LP get left holding the losing side at resolution.

Who takes the riskTHE POOL — passively, with no view, no defense, and no risk manager

Inputs

Why it's rejectedThe price is a function of inventory, not truth. A buyer pushes YES up the curve; the LP passively hands out value as the price moves, then at resolution is stuck holding the worthless losing-side tokens. Across ~25 skewed micro-moments per match this impermanent loss compounds to 15–20% of the pool — and it's structural, not tunable. CPMM also needs price discovery the product already has from live odds, so it's solving a problem Maybe doesn't have.
Recommended — risk-free for the LP

Parimutuel — time-weighted

No LP is the counterparty. Every stake goes into one pot; at resolution the winning side splits the whole pot. In Maybe's time-weighted design, earlier-correct bets get a fatter slice — bet right and fast, win more. The house only earns rake — which means LP capital is never at risk and the pool can't be picked off by informed flow. The trade-off is that it needs two-sided liquidity to form natural odds, so it works best alongside the Game Vault rather than alone.

Who takes the riskNOBODY — players bet each other and the house just rakes, so the LP carries zero counterparty risk

Inputs

Why it's recommended alongside the vaultMechanically the LP is never at risk — the house carries zero counterparty exposure and just collects rake, so informed flow can't bleed the pool the way it does with every AMM. That makes it a strong complement to the Game Vault: the vault provides instant two-sided liquidity, parimutuel adds a risk-free rake pool on top. The one weakness to manage is cold-start: with thin early flow the pot is tiny and payouts look unattractive, so it needs the vault (or seeded liquidity) to bootstrap each market. The time-weighting rewards betting right and fast, which keeps engagement high once the pool fills.
Year 2 layer

CLOB — order book

A market maker posts resting bids and asks because they've priced the event. A taker fills against the best quote. Great economics — when markets live long enough for MMs to react and recover inventory. For 15-second markets, the book is mostly empty.

Who takes the riskINDIVIDUAL MMs — optionally, with their own un-mutualized capital; no MM, no market

Inputs

Why it's the Y2 layer, not launchOne MM quoting fast isn't a book — you need committed competing MMs on both sides, each with $50k–$500k of their own capital, and none of it is mutualised. When the MM's react time eats a big share of a short market, coverage collapses (~30% of sub-minute markets go unquoted). CLOB shines on continuous and long-duration markets and for the US-regulated layer later — not the cold-start MVP.
Rejected — AMM family

LMSR — Logarithmic Market Scoring Rule

Hanson's classic prediction-market AMM. Prices come from a logarithmic cost function with a liquidity parameter b. Bounded LP loss (unlike CPMM) — but still a curve with no real odds, and still picked off on sub-minute sports markets.

Who takes the riskTHE POOL — passively, exposed to bounded loss of b·ln(2) per market, paid in adverse selection by sharps

Inputs

Why it's rejectedLMSR's max loss is bounded at b·ln(2) ≈ 0.693b — that's its big claim to fame over CPMM. But this is a subsidy budget, not an edge: there's no model price feeding the curve, so the AMM lets users buy winning shares cheaply at the start of skewed markets, then pays them at resolution. The fix Manifold uses (play money) doesn't work for a real-money in-play product. Used in production by Manifold Markets, Augur v1, Polymarket pre-CPMM, Gnosis Conditional Tokens (LMSR mode).
Promising — but not for sub-minute

PM-AMM — Paradigm's Gaussian AMM

Paradigm's 2024 design built specifically for prediction markets. The trading curve comes from a Gaussian score model, which concentrates liquidity near the most likely prices and gives a uniform loss-vs-rebalancing (LVR) rate across the whole price range. The first AMM with structural improvements over CPMM/LMSR for prediction markets.

Who takes the riskTHE POOL — but with a uniform LVR shape: at extreme prices it loses much less than CPMM (3% at p=0.99 vs CPMM's 80%)

Inputs

Why it's the strongest pure-AMM option — but still not the launch modelPM-AMM solves the extreme-price bleed that kills CPMM/LMSR: by concentrating liquidity around the most likely price, an LP holding a 0.95 YES side loses ~5% of pool, not 50%. The curve is also model-aware — it knows prediction markets aren't ordinary tokens. For long-duration markets (politics, elections, weather), this is a real upgrade. For sub-minute in-play sports, the same problem remains: the curve has no live odds feed, so it's still a price-taker against informed flow inside a 10-second window. Used in production by Polymarket exploring it, Limitless considering, multiple research deployments.
Best AMM math — wrong product fit

Dynamic PM-AMM — time-decaying liquidity

PM-AMM's killer extension. Liquidity L scales with √(T−t) — the closer to resolution, the smaller the AMM gets. This caps total expected LVR over the market's life: LPs aren't punished by the volatility spike near expiry. Mathematically the cleanest AMM in production today.

Who takes the riskTHE POOL — with the lowest LVR of any AMM. For a 60-min market, total expected loss ≈ 2·L·φ(0) regardless of how prices move

Inputs

Why it's the cleanest math — and still not Maybe's launch modelDynamic PM-AMM is the right answer to "what's the most LP-friendly AMM possible for prediction markets". For markets with hours-to-weeks horizons, the time-decay protects LPs from the volatility spike that destroys other AMMs near expiry. The reason Maybe doesn't lead with it: it's still passive math against informed flow. Sub-minute markets have so little time for arbitrage to settle that even the cleanest AMM can't compete with a vault that actively reprices on a live odds feed. Right answer for long-duration markets in a Year 2 layer. Production: Paradigm reference implementation, several research deployments testing dynamic-L curves on Base.
Considered — vault wins on simplicity

Hybrid AMM + House

Two-tier liquidity. Small bets hit an AMM curve (cheap to operate, always-on, fast UX). Big bets get smart-routed to the house book (model-priced, exposure-managed). Captures AMM's cold-start fix and the house's discipline on size. The cost: two systems to build, two risk models to manage.

Who takes the riskTHE POOL takes both — AMM impermanent loss on small flow + book liability on big flow, with smart-routing deciding which

Inputs

Why considered — and why the pure vault still winsThe smart-routing intuition is right: keep the AMM for retail dust where the LVR is tiny, route real money to a managed book. But the vault already does this by capping exposure per market — the “AMM tier” mostly buys you a way to quote when no one's there to price, which a model-fed odds engine already gives you. Cost-benefit doesn't pay: two codepaths, two settlement flows, two risk teams, for a tier the vault didn't need. Worth revisiting if the product expands to long-duration markets where AMM convenience matters more. Used in production by Polymarket (CPMM AMM + operator-subsidized liquidity), Limitless (Hybrid AMM+CLOB), Stake-style sportsbooks (book + AMM-quoted exotics).
